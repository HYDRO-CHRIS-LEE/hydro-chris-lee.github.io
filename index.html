My Story

I am Jonghyeok Lee, a dedicated hydrologist with a strong background in Civil and Environmental Engineering and Economics. My research bridges the gap between hydrological modeling and economic analysis, focusing on how human activities impact water resources. By developing innovative solutions such as enhancing global water resource assessments with CLM5 and improving streamflow predictions using Graph Neural Networks, I aim to build comprehensive, explainable models that address both environmental and economic challenges.

Land-Surface Model

I work on the Community Land Model(CLM5), with a particular focus on hydrology and water resource processes.

Machine Learning

I apply machine learning techniques, particularly graph neural networks, to improve streamflow prediction by incorporating hydrological insights.

Programming

I utilize a variety of programming languages and tools for scientific computing, model development, and data analysis, using a Linux server environment.

Featured Projects

Incorporating Sectoral Groundwater Withdrawals in CLM5

This project integrates sectoral groundwater withdrawals into the Community Land Model (CLM5) to improve water resource assessments. The project involves developing a base framework for the model and selecting appropriate parameters for different sectors, such as agriculture, industry, and domestic use. The global sectoral water withdrawal results demonstrate how withdrawals affect groundwater recharge and depletion in various regions. This model enhancement allows for more accurate prediction of water shortages and better policy planning for sustainable water use.

Improving Streamflow Prediction with Graph Neural Network

In this project, various graph neural network (GNN) models such as T-GCN, ASTGCN, and Graph WaveNet were applied to predict streamflow more accurately, particularly in regions affected by complex hydrological interactions. The performance box plot illustrates the efficiency of each model in terms of accuracy, and the second image shows how model performance improves as we move downstream in river networks. This approach allows for better flood forecasting and water resource management by utilizing the power of machine learning to capture intricate relationships between hydrological variables and streamflow.

Automating Hydrologically-Informed Adjacency Matrices in ArcGIS

This project automates the generation of hydrologically-informed adjacency matrices in ArcGIS, a critical task for analyzing the spatial relationships between water bodies and drainage networks. The schematic diagram illustrates different types of adjacency matrices used in hydrology, such as direct flow connections and upstream-downstream relationships. The accompanying video demonstrates the automated process, which significantly reduces the time and effort required to create these matrices manually. This tool is essential for enhancing hydrological models and improving decision-making in water resource management.

Measuring the Effects of Changes in Real GDP Caused by Weather Shocks

This project investigates the economic impact of weather shocks, such as changes in temperature and precipitation, on real GDP using a local projection model. The equations and impulse response graphs display how a 10% increase in weather shocks can influence GDP for different countries, including South Korea and France. The results show how temperature and precipitation shocks affect economic growth over time, with confidence intervals highlighting the significance of these effects. This analysis helps in understanding the vulnerability of economies to climate change and guiding adaptation strategies.
